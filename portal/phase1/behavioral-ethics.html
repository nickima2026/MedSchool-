Landmark Article
The Belmont Report: Ethical Principles and Guidelines for the Protection of Human Subjects of Research
National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects
Federal Register, 1979
Why This Paper Matters
The foundational document in research ethics that every physician must know. Established the three core principles—respect for persons, beneficence, and justice—that govern all human subjects research and inform clinical ethics to this day.
Key Takeaways for Rounds
1
Respect for Persons: Individuals should be treated as autonomous agents; persons with diminished autonomy are entitled to protection.
2
Beneficence: Obligation to maximize benefits and minimize harm—the basis of risk-benefit analysis in research and clinical practice.
3
Justice: Fair distribution of the burdens and benefits of research—directly addressing exploitation of vulnerable populations (as seen in the Tuskegee Study).
4
Informed Consent: Must include disclosure of information, comprehension by the subject, and voluntariness—the gold standard still used today.
★
Pimp Pearl: An attending may ask ‘What are the three principles of the Belmont Report?’—know them cold.
